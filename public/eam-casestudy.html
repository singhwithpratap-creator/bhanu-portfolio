00 — Context

The challenge

Meridian Industries operates 15 manufacturing plants across North America, managing over 12,000 physical assets ranging from industrial pumps to HVAC systems and electrical transformers.

Their legacy EAM system — a 9-year-old on-premise Maximo deployment — was creating cascading operational failures. Maintenance crews were drowning in paperwork, managers couldn't get real-time visibility, and critical asset failures were going undetected until production halted.

Before — The Status Quo
  • ✕Avg. 12 min to create a single work order
  • ✕Mobile task completion rate of only 34%
  • ✕4.2 hour mean time to repair (MTTR)
  • ✕Zero predictive capability — all reactive
Target State
  • ✓Work order creation under 3 minutes
  • ✓85%+ mobile task completion rate
  • ✓MTTR under 2.8 hours
  • ✓AI-driven predictive maintenance alerts

Key research insights

I1
The 10-second rule. Field technicians won't tolerate more than 10 seconds to load or navigate to any screen. They're standing in noisy, high-temperature environments with dirty gloves. Speed is survival.
I2
Managers live in reports, techs live in tasks. These two user groups have almost no UI overlap in their needs. A unified dashboard must serve both without compromising either.
I3
Trust in AI is earned, not assumed. Technicians were skeptical of "AI recommendations." They needed to see why the AI flagged something before they'd act on it. Explainability is critical.
I4
Offline isn't optional. Plants have dead spots. The system must work without connectivity and sync when connection is restored. Techs in basement pump rooms need it most.

03 — Ideate

From problems to possibilities

We ran two full-day design sprints with cross-functional teams in Mural, generating over 200 ideas before converging on 14 key design directions.

How Might We statements

The six HMW questions that shaped our entire design direction — developed from research synthesis in Mural.

HMW help technicians complete work orders in under 3 minutes, even in noisy, low-connectivity environments?
HMW give operations managers real-time visibility without requiring them to refresh dashboards constantly?
HMW make AI recommendations feel trustworthy and explainable to skeptical maintenance veterans?
HMW reduce duplicate work orders to near-zero without adding friction to the creation process?
HMW help plant directors get compliance-ready reports in seconds instead of hours?
HMW surface the right information to the right person at exactly the right time — without information overload?

Key design decisions

Each decision came from research evidence, not assumptions. We documented "before vs. after" for every major choice.

1
Rejected approach
Single-view dashboard that tries to serve all users simultaneously with toggle filters
Chosen approach
Role-based dashboard with a unified shell but context-aware content prioritization based on logged-in user type
2
Rejected approach
Full-page AI recommendations requiring users to navigate to a dedicated "AI Insights" page
Chosen approach
Inline AI Insights panel directly on the dashboard with one-click actions — reducing clicks from 4+ to 1
3
Rejected approach
Traditional form-based work order creation with 14 required fields
Chosen approach
Progressive disclosure — 3 required fields minimum, smart defaults from AI, optional fields collapsed but accessible
4
Rejected approach
Show all AI confidence scores and model outputs — "full transparency"
Chosen approach
Plain language explanations ("Pump P-2847 vibration 23% above baseline") with optional technical detail behind a "See why" link

Claude AI in the design process

We used Claude throughout the design process — not to replace design thinking, but to accelerate it. Here's where it added the most value.

🤖
Generating copy variations. For every label, button, error message, and tooltip, Claude generated 5 variants in seconds. We tested them in Maze. Time saved: ~12 hours of copywriting.
⚡
Rapid HTML/CSS prototyping. Claude generated working code prototypes for complex interactions (the AI Insights panel, tab switching) in minutes. Engineers could evaluate feasibility immediately.
🔍
Accessibility auditing. We prompted Claude to review every screen for WCAG 2.1 AA compliance — identifying 14 contrast issues and 8 missing ARIA labels before dev handoff.
📋
Research synthesis. 26 interview transcripts analyzed in Dovetail, then summarized by Claude into theme clusters. Reduced synthesis time from 3 days to 6 hours.

Round 1 findings & how we fixed them

Critical: "Flagged by AI" tab was completely missed by 9/12 participants
Users didn't notice the third tab in the Work Orders section. All 9 said they'd "never look there" unless it was highlighted or on the main dashboard.
Fix → Added AI flagged count badge to the tab label. Added a persistent NexusAI Insights panel directly on the main dashboard. Tab retained for power users.
Critical: Notification bell dropdown was invisible to 7/12 users
The badge count was too small and lacked contrast in light mode. Several participants said they expected a sidebar notification, not a top-right dropdown.
Fix → Increased badge size, added a red pulse animation for unread alerts. Added "7 new notifications" text visible on hover for screen readers and low-vision users.
Major: Work order detail drawer was too wide on tablet screens
At 480px, the slide-in drawer covered nearly the entire viewport on a 10-inch tablet, blocking all context. Technicians using tablets in the field were frustrated.
Fix → Added responsive breakpoint: drawer becomes full-screen modal on viewports under 640px with a clear "back" navigation instead of X button.
Major: AI recommendation language was too technical for field technicians
"Vibration readings 23% above baseline" was understood by 4/6 technicians, but "predictive failure window: 14 days" caused confusion — did that mean it would definitely fail?
Fix → Revised to plain language: "This pump could fail within 14 days. Inspection recommended." Added optional "Technical details" expandable for engineers.
Minor: Date pill in welcome header was confused with a button
4 participants tried to click the date pill, expecting to open a calendar or date-filter. It's decorative but looked interactive.
Fix → Removed hover state and pointer cursor from the date pill. Added `cursor: default` and removed border on hover to signal non-interactivity.

07 — Reflection

What I'd do differently

Honest retrospective on the design process — what worked, what didn't, and what I'd change.

⚡
Test earlier with lower fidelity
We waited until mid-fi wireframes to run formal usability testing. In hindsight, 5-minute paper prototype tests in Week 4 would have caught the AI tab discoverability issue 8 weeks earlier.
🏭
Bring engineers to the field
When our lead engineer spent a day on the plant floor, she redesigned the offline sync architecture from scratch — in a good way. Engineers on research trips = fewer "that's not technically possible" conversations.
🤖
AI needs an explainability-first strategy
We designed the AI features before defining how they'd explain themselves. Rebuilding the AI Insights copy and interaction model in Week 10 cost us two weeks. Define AI communication patterns before the first wireframe.
